# Framing Correction — 2026-04-23 (post-Phase G)

**Read this before any other investigation document.** Earlier docs (especially those written between Phase D and Phase G's completion) used a framing that conflated three distinct things. This document captures the corrected understanding.

---

## The error in earlier framing

Earlier I wrote things like "29h is RETRACTED — overestimates literature" and "FBA contributes zero temporal content." That framing conflated:

1. **What the model predicts under its assumptions** (29h — mathematically correct)
2. **What real isolated mitochondria do** (1-18h — empirical literature)
3. **Whether the model "matches" reality** (incomplete framing — depends on scope)

A model can be **correct in its predictions** while **incomplete in its scope**. The 29h is what our framework predicts under the specific assumptions of (uniform protein decay, 5% buffer, 20% threshold, no other failure modes). The literature gives 1-18h because real mitochondria have OTHER failure modes that our model doesn't include.

**The gap between 29h and the literature is not "the model being wrong" — it's "the model capturing one mechanism, reality having multiple."**

---

## The corrected framing

**29h = the protein-decay-only ceiling.** This is what transit window would be if the only failure mode was nuclear protein turnover. It's a mathematically correct prediction under the model's assumptions.

**4-18h = empirical reality.** This is what real isolated mitochondria do under various preservation conditions. The discrepancy with 29h tells us non-protein-decay failure modes (membrane damage, ROS, MPTP, cyt c release) dominate current preservation protocols.

**The gap = engineering opportunity.** If you address the failure modes our model doesn't include (membrane stabilization, antioxidants, MPTP inhibition), you could approach the protein-decay-limited ceiling. That's a testable, actionable prediction.

---

## What's correct, what's incomplete, what's not in scope

### Correct (within model assumptions)
- **TW = -t½ × log₂(threshold) × buffer_factor** under uniform decay → 29h at t½=12h, threshold=20%, buffer=1.05
- **TW shortens with protein heterogeneity via order statistics on AND-clauses** — 8h at lognormal σ=0.6
- **All ETC complexes saturate together at the threshold** — mathematically inevitable under uniform flux-relative decay
- **First-failure reaction is whatever has highest baseline-to-cap utilization** — PIt2mB_mitoMap in our setup
- **The 145-gene essential set, 89% mitochondrial GO** — biologically real, externally validated

### Out of scope (not in the model)
- Membrane integrity / cardiolipin peroxidation
- Outer membrane permeabilization / cyt c release
- Mitochondrial permeability transition pore (MPTP) opening
- Ca²⁺ overload
- Lipid peroxidation
- Aggregate-mediated misfolding
- Direct oxidative damage to ETC complexes (we model ROS production at CI but not damage from it, except via the optional G.5 coupling)

### What FBA actually contributes
The framework's substantive structural contributions are:
1. **Identification of the 145-gene essential set** (FBA-derived, validated by GO)
2. **Identification that the largest required AND-clause governs effective decay** (CI = 39 subunits in MitoMAMMAL — order statistics minimum is dominated by the largest clause)
3. **A platform for adding mechanistic extensions** (e.g., the ROS coupling test in Phase G.5 worked using the model's existing o2s_m production)

What FBA does NOT contribute:
- The temporal dynamics under uniform decay — that's `-log₂(threshold)` algebra
- The temporal dynamics under heterogeneity — that's order statistics × `-log₂(threshold)`
- A novel transit window number per se — the number is structurally determined by the largest AND-clause

## Correction 3: Order-statistics independence assumption is unverified

**Earlier claim:** "TW = -log₂(threshold) × E[min over N=39 samples] matches FBA within 0.6h"

**The math is correct conditional on independence.** But CI subunits are co-translationally assembled with NDUFAFx chaperones — their effective half-lives are correlated, not independent. "Min over 39 i.i.d. samples" overestimates the dispersion of the minimum.

In reality, CI may behave more like a single entity with one effective t½ (set by the slowest-assembling subunit or chaperone). The numerical agreement with our FBA simulation (which ALSO assumes independence in the lognormal sampling) doesn't validate the biological independence assumption.

**The cleanest claim:** "Under the assumption of independent per-subunit decay, order statistics on the largest AND-clause governs effective TW. Whether real subunits decay independently is an open question."

## Correction 4: ROS coupling / heterogeneity convergence is sweep observation, not independent confirmation

**Earlier claim:** "Two mechanisms converge on 8h, validating the realistic prediction"

**More honest:** σ=0.6 and k=3 both lying in "biologically plausible midpoint" regions of their respective sweeps and producing similar TW is suggestive, not confirmatory. Neither parameter is independently calibrated to a specific dataset. The convergence is interesting but doesn't constitute independent validation.

## Correction 5: "Engineering opportunity" claim needs care

**Earlier claim:** "Engineering interventions targeting non-proteomic failure modes (membrane, ROS, MPTP) could close the 14h gap toward the 29h ceiling."

**What's defensible (structural):** If the model gives 29h under protein decay alone and empirical gives 4-18h, then either (a) protein decay is faster than t½=12h in real isolated mitos, or (b) non-protein-decay mechanisms dominate. That's a falsifiable disjunction.

# Mechanism: Why Ablating 229 Genes Collapses ATP, and What That Actually Means

This section explains both the FBA mechanism behind the synthetic-essentiality finding and whether the ablation test is biologically meaningful for the transit-window question. It exists because the three-class result (Correction 1 above) is easy to misread.

## Why ATP collapses

The model's GPR rules describe enzyme requirements. Common patterns:
- `(A and B and C)` — all three subunits needed (AND)
- `(A or B)` — either alternative works (OR)
- `(A or B or C)` — three alternatives

A gene's individual KO has small impact when:
- It's in an OR clause with other genes (alternatives compensate)
- The reaction isn't on the optimal pathway (network reroutes around it)
- It catalyzes a step that has multiple paths

So if reaction R has GPR `(geneA or geneB or geneC)` and all three exist, knocking out A alone barely matters because B and C still work. **A is "individually dispensable."**

But knocking out A AND B AND C together kills R entirely. The "individual dispensability" was conditional on the other alternatives being present.

The 229 contains many such OR-alternatives. Ablating them all simultaneously kills lots of reactions because each one's "alternative" was also in the 229.

This is **synthetic essentiality** — a real biological phenomenon (cancer drugs exploit it), not just a model artifact.

## Two different questions, two different relevance

### Question A: "What's the minimal viable mitochondrion?"

This is what the ablation test addresses. **Answer:** NOT just the 145. You need 145 essentials + at least one representative from each synthetic-essential OR-cluster. Probably ~352 of the 374.

### Question B: "What governs the transit window during uniform decay?"

This is the actual question for our work. Under transit, **ALL 374 genes decay TOGETHER** at the same rate. The 145 essentials decay just as fast as the 229. So the 229 being "collectively essential" matters less here — they're all decaying together anyway.

For the transit question, what matters is: **which AND-clause loses enough capacity first?** That's still CI's 39-subunit AND-clause (under heterogeneity) or "all reactions hit threshold simultaneously" (under uniform decay).

## What the ablation test actually tells us about transit

The 145 / 229 split is not a "minimal essential set" finding — it's a **sensitivity tier** finding.

For the transit window specifically:
- All 374 decay together
- The 145 are MORE SENSITIVE (smaller perturbations affect them)
- The 229 are LESS SENSITIVE individually but still part of the network
- The transit-window math (CI = 39 = largest AND-clause) doesn't depend on this split

## What was wrong with the earlier framing

Earlier docs said "minimal essential set of 145 sustains ATP." That conflated two different concepts:
- **Single-KO essentiality** (which the 145 measures)
- **Minimal viable set** (which is bigger because of synthetic essentiality)

The ablation test caught this conflation. But the transit-window claim doesn't actually depend on it — for transit, all genes decay together and CI's AND-clause is the rate-limiter regardless.

## Bottom line

The ablation test is a useful sanity check but not directly relevant to the transit window question. It corrected a sloppy claim ("minimal essential set sustains ATP") but doesn't change the actual transit-window finding.

The honest claims are:

1. **145 individually-essential genes** — these are the most sensitive to perturbation
2. **The other 229 are network redundancy** — individually replaceable, collectively required (~22 truly redundant, ~207 synthetically essential)
3. **For transit (uniform decay), CI's 39-subunit AND-clause governs the window** — the 145/229 split is mostly orthogonal to this
4. **The minimal viable set** (what you'd need to engineer a working mitochondrion de novo) is ~350 genes, not 145
